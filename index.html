ComaEval

Coma: Unarousable unresponsiveness with eyes closed (from Greek "koma" = deep sleep).

States of Arousal: Alert → Descriptive for in-between → Coma.

Anatomy of Arousal: Requires intact upper brainstem, bilateral thalami, and bilateral cerebral hemispheres.

Record Sedation: e.g., "Propofol 1.5 mg/min stopped 20 min prior."

Baseline Arousal

  • Eyes: Open/closed?
  • Spontaneous movements?
  • Regard/track gaze?

Stimuli for Maximal Arousal

Escalate: Talk → Loud → Yell → Shake → Noxious stimulus.

The Bedside Neurological Examination of the Unresponsive Patient: A Clinical Guide

Immediate Bedside Priorities: Stabilize Before You Examine

The evaluation of an unresponsive patient is a medical emergency that demands a structured, prioritized approach. Before any detailed neurological assessment can be performed, life-threatening conditions must be identified and managed. The primary principle is to treat the patient, not just the neurological signs.

Assess and Stabilize: The ABCs

  • A - Airway: Ensure patency. Consider early intubation.
  • B - Breathing: Assess respiratory status. Provide supplemental oxygen.
  • C - Circulation: Check pulse and blood pressure. Treat hypotension aggressively.
  • C-Spine - Cervical Spine Precautions: Immobilize if trauma is known or suspected.

Check for and Treat Readily Reversible Causes

  • Hypoglycemia: Check finger-stick glucose. Administer D50 if low.
    • Clinical Pearl: Give thiamine before or with dextrose in at-risk patients to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
  • Toxidromes: Consider opioid overdose (coma, respiratory depression, pinpoint pupils) and treat with naloxone. Use flumazenil for benzodiazepine overdose with extreme caution.

Essential Preliminaries for the Neurological Exam

  • Record Sedation: Note the type, dose, and time of all sedatives.
  • Gather History: From EMS, family, or witnesses.
  • Initial Labs and Imaging: Get labs (metabolic panel, CBC, etc.) and an emergent non-contrast head CT.

Detailed Neurological Examination: Brainstem and Cranial Nerves

Pupils (CN II - Afferent, CN III - Efferent)

Assess size, shape, and reaction to light (direct and consensual). A unilateral dilated, fixed pupil is a neurosurgical emergency.

Table 2: Pupillary Findings and Localization

Ocular Motility (CN III, IV, VI, VIII)

  • Oculocephalic Reflex (Doll's Eyes):

    CONTRAINDICATED if C-spine injury is suspected.

    Turn head; intact reflex means eyes move opposite to head turn.
  • Oculovestibular Reflex (Cold Calorics):

    NEVER in an awake patient.

    Irrigate ear with cold water; intact reflex means eyes deviate toward the irrigated ear.

Facial Sensation and Motor (CN V, VII)

  • Corneal Reflex: Touch cornea with saline or cotton wisp. Normal response is a bilateral blink.

Lower Cranial Nerves (CN IX, X)

  • Gag Reflex: Stimulate posterior pharynx. Often unreliable.
  • Cough Reflex: Suction endotracheal tube. More reliable test of lower brainstem function in intubated patients.

Motor Examination: Purposeful vs. Posturing

Differentiate between purposeful movements (obeys commands, localizes, withdraws) and reflexive posturing (decorticate, decerebrate).

  • Decorticate Posturing (GCS M3): Flexion of arms, extension of legs. Lesion above the red nucleus.
  • Decerebrate Posturing (GCS M2): Extension of arms and legs. More ominous; indicates a brainstem lesion.

Documentation and Communication

Be descriptive. Avoid ambiguous terms like "lethargic." Always record sedation status. A clear note might read: "GCS 7T (E2, V-T, M4). Eyes open to pain. Intubated. Withdraws all limbs to nailbed pressure symmetrically."
